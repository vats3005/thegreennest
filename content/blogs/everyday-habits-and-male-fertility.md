When couples come to see me for a fertility consultation, our conversation almost always centers on the woman first — her cycle, her hormones, her ovarian reserve. But fertility is a shared biological story, and the male partner's everyday habits matter just as much as any test result. In my 24 years of practice, I have watched simple, sustained changes in daily routine make a genuine difference alongside medical treatment. This is not about blame or pressure. It is about understanding what is within your control, and what is not.

## Sperm Health Is Always a Work in Progress

One of the most reassuring things I tell my male patients is that sperm are constantly being produced. Unlike a woman's egg supply, which is largely fixed from birth, sperm cells regenerate on an ongoing cycle that takes roughly three months from start to maturity. This means that the habits a man practices today will influence the sperm being formed months from now. It also means there is a genuine window of opportunity — changes made now can meaningfully shape what shows up in a semen analysis taken later in the year.

## Weight, Metabolism and Hormonal Balance

Body weight and fertility are more connected than most people realize. Carrying excess weight, particularly around the abdomen, can quietly disrupt the hormonal signals that support healthy sperm production. Fat tissue can alter the balance between testosterone and estrogen in ways that are not always obvious from the outside. On the other end of the spectrum, being significantly underweight or engaging in extreme, prolonged endurance exercise can also interfere with hormonal rhythm. The goal is not a particular number on a scale, but a stable, moderate level of physical activity and a body composition that feels sustainable, not extreme in either direction.

## Heat, Habits and the Environment Around You

The testes sit outside the body for a biological reason — they need to stay slightly cooler than core body temperature to function well. Anything that consistently raises that temperature can affect sperm quality over time. This includes frequent hot tub or sauna use, laptops balanced directly on the lap for extended periods, and very tight-fitting underwear worn daily. None of these need to become a source of anxiety; occasional exposure is not the concern. It is the sustained, daily pattern that adds up. Small, practical swaps — looser-fitting clothing, a desk instead of a lap for laptop use, moderating sauna sessions — are simple changes with real potential benefit.

## What You Eat and Drink Matters More Than You Think

A diet rich in antioxidants — found in colourful vegetables, fruits, nuts and seeds — helps protect sperm from oxidative stress, a natural process that can damage sperm DNA over time. I encourage patients to think of their plate the same way they think of any investment: consistency matters more than perfection. On the other side, heavy alcohol use, smoking and recreational substance use are among the most well-documented disruptors of sperm count, movement and shape. Even moderate but regular alcohol intake can have a measurable effect on hormonal balance. Quitting smoking and reducing alcohol are two of the most impactful changes a man can make, and the earlier these changes begin, the more time the body has to benefit before a treatment cycle.

## Sleep, Stress and the Hormones You Cannot See

Testosterone production follows a daily rhythm that is closely tied to sleep. Poor sleep quality or chronically short sleep can lower testosterone levels and, over time, affect sperm production. Chronic stress works through a related but separate pathway, raising cortisol in ways that can interfere with the hormonal signals the testes rely on. Neither sleep nor stress management are quick fixes, but they are foundational. I often suggest patients treat sleep as seriously as they would treat a supplement or medication — protecting seven to eight hours a night is not indulgent, it is physiological support for fertility.

## Bringing It Into the Clinical Picture

None of this means lifestyle changes alone will resolve every fertility challenge — many causes of male infertility are structural, genetic or require medical intervention that lifestyle changes cannot substitute for. But when lifestyle factors are contributing, even partially, addressing them alongside medical evaluation gives your body the best possible foundation.
