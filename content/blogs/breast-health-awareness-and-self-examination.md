## Why Breast Health Deserves a Place in Your Routine

In my consultations, breast health often comes up almost as an afterthought — tucked in at the end of a conversation about periods, fertility, or general wellness. I always bring it back to the centre of the discussion, because knowing your own body is one of the simplest and most empowering forms of self-care available to you. Breast tissue changes throughout life — with your menstrual cycle, with age, with pregnancy and breastfeeding, and around menopause — and learning what is normal *for you* is far more useful than comparing yourself to anyone else's experience. This isn't about vigilance or worry. It's about familiarity, so that if something does feel different, you notice it early and calmly, and know exactly who to call.

## What "Normal" Actually Looks Like

One of the most reassuring things I tell my patients is that there is no single template for normal breast tissue. Some women have naturally lumpy or dense breasts; others have tissue that feels smoother and more uniform. Breasts are often not identical to each other, and that in itself is completely ordinary. What matters is establishing your own baseline — how your breasts typically feel and look at different points in your cycle — so that a genuine change stands out against that personal backdrop rather than against some external standard. Hormonal fluctuations can make breasts feel fuller, tender, or slightly more nodular in the days before your period, and this cyclical variation is expected and not a cause for concern on its own.

## Building a Simple Self-Examination Habit

Self-examination doesn't need to be clinical or intimidating. I encourage my patients to choose a consistent time — a few days after your period ends, when hormonal swelling has settled, is often the easiest time to feel changes clearly. If your cycles are irregular or you're past menopause, simply picking the same date each month works well.

The habit itself has three gentle parts. First, a visual check in front of a mirror, with arms relaxed and then raised, looking for any changes in shape, size, skin texture, or the appearance of the nipple. Second, a physical check lying down, using the flat pads of your fingers to move in small circular motions across the entire breast and up into the underarm area, covering the tissue in a methodical pattern so nothing is missed. Third, the same physical check standing or sitting, which some women find easiest to do in the shower when skin is slippery and tissue is easier to feel. None of this needs to take more than a few minutes, and with practice it becomes second nature rather than a chore.

## What You're Actually Feeling For

The goal is not to diagnose anything yourself — it's to notice change. Things worth paying attention to include a new lump or thickened area that feels different from the surrounding tissue and persists beyond a cycle, changes in the size or shape of one breast compared to the other, dimpling or puckering of the skin, changes to the nipple such as new inversion or unusual discharge, persistent skin changes like redness, scaling, or unusual warmth, or new and persistent discomfort in one specific area rather than general cyclical tenderness. Finding something doesn't mean something serious is happening — most changes people notice through self-examination turn out to be benign, cyclical, or related to entirely ordinary tissue variation. But noticing gives you the chance to have it properly evaluated rather than wondering quietly.

## When to Bring It to a Clinical Visit

I always tell patients: trust your own observation over your own diagnosis. If you notice any of the changes above, or simply feel unsure about something, that's reason enough to come in — you don't need to wait for it to feel dramatic or urgent. A clinical breast examination complements what you do at home and adds a trained, external perspective, and depending on your age and personal history, your gynaecologist may also discuss imaging as part of your broader wellness picture. Self-examination is not a replacement for professional care; it's a way of staying attentive between visits and bringing useful, specific information when you do come in. Telling your doctor "I noticed this change starting around three weeks ago, and it's stayed the same since" is far more useful than a general sense that something feels off, and self-examination is what gives you that clarity.

## Making It Part of Ongoing Wellness Care

At The Green Nest, we see breast health as one thread in a larger fabric of wellness that includes your cycle, your hormonal health, and your long-term wellbeing — not a separate, isolated concern. I encourage every patient, regardless of age or life stage, to fold self-examination into an existing routine, whether that's a monthly reminder on your phone or simply tying it to the end of your period. Pair it with your annual gynaecological check-up, where a clinical examination and any relevant screening can be discussed based on your individual history and risk factors. Caring for your breast health doesn't require anxiety or a rigid checklist — it asks for gentle, regular attention, the same way you'd check in on any other part of your body you care about. Over time, this quiet habit becomes one of the most grounding forms of self-knowledge you can carry with you.
