Almost every woman I meet has, at some point, said some version of the same thing to me: "I don't feel like myself in the week before my period." Irritability that seems to arrive out of nowhere, a heaviness in the chest, tears over something that wouldn't normally bother you, or simply wanting to cancel every plan and stay in bed. For most women, this is premenstrual syndrome, or PMS — a familiar, manageable part of the monthly cycle. For a smaller group, these changes are far more intense and disruptive, and fall into a distinct condition called premenstrual dysphoric disorder, or PMDD. Neither is "in your head." Both are real, hormonally driven, and worth understanding properly.

## What's Happening Hormonally Each Month

Your menstrual cycle isn't just about ovulation and bleeding — it's a month-long conversation between your ovaries and your brain. In the days after ovulation, progesterone rises and then falls sharply just before your period begins, while estrogen follows a similar dip. These hormones don't just act on the uterus; they also interact directly with brain chemicals that regulate mood, particularly serotonin. When progesterone and estrogen drop, serotonin activity can dip along with them. For most women, the brain adjusts to this monthly shift without much trouble. For others, the nervous system seems to be more sensitive to these hormonal swings, and that sensitivity is what drives the emotional symptoms of PMS and, more intensely, PMDD.

## PMS vs PMDD: Knowing the Difference

It helps to think of PMS and PMDD as sitting on the same spectrum, but at very different points of intensity. PMS typically involves mild to moderate symptoms — some bloating, breast tenderness, fatigue, mild irritability or low mood — that show up in the one to two weeks before your period and ease once bleeding starts. Most women manage these symptoms without much disruption to daily life.

PMDD is a different experience altogether. The mood symptoms are far more severe: intense irritability or anger, deep sadness or hopelessness, anxiety that feels hard to switch off, or a sense of being overwhelmed and out of control. These symptoms are significant enough to interfere with work, relationships, or day-to-day functioning, and they follow a clear pattern — appearing in the days before your period and resolving within a few days of it starting. If this monthly pattern sounds familiar and it's affecting how you function, it's worth having a proper conversation about it rather than simply pushing through.

## Common Signs to Watch For

While every woman's experience is different, some patterns tend to repeat. Physically, you might notice bloating, breast tenderness, headaches, joint or muscle aches, and changes in sleep or appetite. Emotionally, the signs often include irritability or anger that feels disproportionate to the situation, tearfulness, anxiety, low motivation, difficulty concentrating, and a general sense of being "on edge." The key clue that ties these together, and that separates them from other mood conditions, is timing: symptoms cluster in the luteal phase — the back half of your cycle — and lift once your period arrives.

## Why It Happens — The Hormone-Brain Connection

Researchers don't yet fully understand why some women experience PMS or PMDD more intensely than others, but it isn't simply a matter of having "more" or "less" hormone fluctuation. Most women with PMDD actually have hormone levels within a normal range — what seems to differ is how sensitively their brain responds to the normal rise and fall of estrogen and progesterone across the cycle. Genetics, existing anxiety or mood conditions, high stress levels, and sleep disruption can all make this sensitivity more pronounced. This is important to know, because it means these symptoms are a genuine physiological response, not a character flaw or a lack of willpower.

## Ways to Feel More in Control

There's a lot that can help, and it doesn't have to mean waiting it out every month. Tracking your cycle alongside your symptoms — even in a simple notes app — is one of the most useful first steps, because it turns a vague sense of "I feel awful sometimes" into a clear, predictable pattern you can plan around and discuss with your doctor. Regular movement, consistent sleep, and reducing caffeine and alcohol in the days before your period can ease physical symptoms for many women. Stress-management practices, whether that's breathing exercises, journaling, or simply protecting some quiet time in your calendar during the luteal phase, can make a genuine difference to the emotional side of things. For PMS, lifestyle measures are often enough. For PMDD, they're usually only part of the picture, and additional support — sometimes involving targeted hormonal or non-hormonal treatment options tailored to you — can help considerably.

## When to Seek Support

If your premenstrual symptoms are mild and manageable, that's a normal part of many women's cycles and doesn't necessarily need treatment beyond simple lifestyle care. But if you notice a consistent monthly pattern of mood changes that feel severe, that affect your relationships or work, or that leave you feeling like you lose weeks of your life every month, please don't dismiss it as something you simply have to live with. A proper evaluation can help distinguish PMS from PMDD from other conditions that might be overlapping with your cycle, and from there, we can build a plan that actually fits your life. You know your own patterns better than anyone — trust that instinct enough to bring it to a conversation.
