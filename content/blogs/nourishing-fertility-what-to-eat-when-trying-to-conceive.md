When couples come to see me at The Green Nest, one of the questions I hear most often is simple: "Doctor, what should I be eating?" It is a fair question, and an important one. While no single food or diet can replace personalised medical care, the everyday choices you make at the table genuinely matter for reproductive health. Over the years, I have seen how thoughtful, sustainable nutrition habits, paired with proper clinical guidance, help create a stronger foundation for the journey ahead. Today, I want to walk you through what a fertility-supportive plate can look like, in a way that feels achievable rather than overwhelming.

## Why What You Eat Matters for Fertility

Your reproductive system does not exist in isolation. It is closely connected to your metabolism, your hormone balance, and your overall inflammation levels, all of which are influenced by diet. Ovulation, egg quality, sperm health, and the uterine environment all rely on steady nourishment. This does not mean you need a rigid or restrictive plan. Rather, it means building habits that keep your blood sugar stable, your body well nourished, and your hormones supported over time. Think of nutrition as one supportive pillar among several, working alongside good sleep, movement, and the care you receive from your clinical team.

## Building a Fertility-Friendly Plate

A helpful way to think about meals is in terms of balance rather than restriction. A fertility-friendly plate typically includes a good portion of colourful vegetables and fruits, a source of quality protein, whole grains or complex carbohydrates, and healthy fats. This combination helps slow down sugar spikes, keeps energy levels steadier through the day, and provides the wide range of vitamins and minerals your reproductive system relies on. I often encourage couples to think in terms of variety across the week rather than perfection at every single meal. A colourful, varied plate most days is far more sustainable, and far more useful, than an all-or-nothing approach.

## Protein, Healthy Fats, and Whole Grains

Protein plays a quiet but essential role in hormone production and tissue repair, so including sources such as lentils, beans, eggs, paneer, fish, or lean poultry regularly can be helpful. Healthy fats, found in nuts, seeds, olive oil, and fatty fish, support the production of reproductive hormones and help your body absorb certain vitamins. Whole grains such as brown rice, whole wheat, millets, and oats provide steady energy and fibre, which supports digestion and helps maintain more stable insulin levels. Swapping refined grains for whole ones is one of the simplest, most sustainable changes couples can make.

## Micronutrients Worth Prioritising

Certain nutrients come up often in conversations about fertility. Folate, found in leafy greens, lentils, and fortified foods, supports early development and is something we discuss with every woman planning a pregnancy. Iron, found in foods like spinach, dals, and lean meats, supports healthy ovulation and energy levels. Vitamin D, obtained through safe sun exposure and foods like eggs and fortified milk, plays a role in hormonal balance for both partners. Omega-3 fatty acids, found in fish, walnuts, and flaxseeds, support egg quality and help manage inflammation. Rather than reaching for supplements on your own, I always recommend discussing your specific needs with your doctor, since individual requirements vary and blood tests can guide what is actually necessary for you.

## Foods and Habits to Approach Mindfully

Just as important as what to add is what to approach with a little more care. Ultra-processed foods, foods high in added sugar, and trans fats found in some fried and packaged items can contribute to inflammation and less stable blood sugar, so it helps to enjoy them mindfully rather than regularly. Alcohol and excess caffeine are also worth moderating while trying to conceive, and this applies to both partners, not just the woman. Male fertility is influenced by diet too, so I always encourage couples to approach these changes as a team. None of this needs to feel punishing. The goal is a pattern that feels sustainable for your lifestyle, not a list of forbidden foods that adds stress to an already emotional time.

## Small Changes, Steady Progress

Perhaps the most important thing I tell my patients is this: you do not need to overhaul your entire diet overnight. Fertility nutrition works best as a gradual, steady practice, not a crash plan. Start with one or two changes, such as adding a serving of vegetables to lunch, swapping white rice for brown rice a few times a week, or replacing a sugary snack with a handful of nuts. These small, consistent choices add up over the weeks and months of your journey. Pair them with regular check-ins with your fertility team, so that your nutrition plan can be tailored to any specific conditions, test results, or treatment protocols you may be following.
